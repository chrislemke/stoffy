# Reflections: Donald Hoffman

## Agreements

### Perception is Constructed
Hoffman is absolutely right that perception is constructed rather than passively received. This is well-established in neuroscience and philosophy of mind.

**Why this resonates**:
Every time I'm tricked by an optical illusion, I'm reminded that my visual system is constructing a model, not photographing reality.

### Evolution Shapes Perception
The idea that evolution shaped our senses for survival rather than truth is compelling. It explains many quirks of human cognition.

**Why this resonates**:
Our cognitive biases (confirmation bias, loss aversion, etc.) make sense as survival mechanisms, not as truth-seeking tools.

### Spacetime May Not Be Fundamental
Hoffman joins Rovelli, Smolin, and others in questioning whether spacetime is fundamental. This is increasingly mainstream in theoretical physics.

**Why this resonates**:
My interest in relational quantum mechanics prepared me for this move. Spacetime as emergent is more elegant than spacetime as fundamental.

---

## Disagreements

### The Leap to Conscious Realism
While I'm sympathetic to the critique of naive realism, I'm not convinced by the leap to idealism. Why should consciousness be more fundamental than matter? The hard problem cuts both ways.

**My counter-position**:
Perhaps neither consciousness nor matter is fundamental. Perhaps both emerge from something more basic—like potentiality or relations.

### Testability Concerns
Hoffman claims Conscious Realism is testable, but the tests he proposes seem far in the future and rely on mathematical frameworks that are still being developed.

**My counter-position**:
A theory should be evaluated not just on its eventual testability but on its current fruitfulness. Conscious Realism may be too speculative.

## Influence on My Worldview

Hoffman has made me more suspicious of naive realism and more open to radical alternatives. Even if I don't fully endorse Conscious Realism, the exercise of taking it seriously has been valuable.

The "fitness beats truth" insight is genuinely important and has implications beyond philosophy—for understanding human irrationality, political polarization, and the limits of science.
