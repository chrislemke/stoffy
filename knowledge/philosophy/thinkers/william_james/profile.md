# Thinker Profile: William James

**Type**: Philosopher, Psychologist
**Era**: Modern (1842-1910)
**Traditions**: Pragmatism, Radical Empiricism, Functional Psychology

## Core Ideas

- **Pragmatic Theory of Truth**: Truth is not a static correspondence between idea and reality but is made true by the difference it makes in practical experience. True ideas are those that work - that prove useful in navigating the world.

- **Stream of Consciousness**: Consciousness is not composed of discrete mental atoms but flows as a continuous stream. This insight, fundamental to his psychology, influenced both cognitive science and literary modernism.

- **Radical Empiricism**: Experience is the ultimate reality, and relations between things are as experienceable as the things themselves. There is no need for a transcendent mind-stuff or matter-stuff; pure experience is the fundamental reality.

- **The Will to Believe**: In genuine options where evidence is inconclusive, we have the right to believe based on our passional nature. Faith in certain matters (like free will, religious belief) can be legitimate even without sufficient evidence.

- **Pluralistic Universe**: Reality is not a finished, monistic whole but an unfinished, pluralistic multiverse in the making. There is genuine novelty, chance, and multiple centers of experience.

- **Soft Determinism and Free Will**: James defended free will as compatible with a scientific worldview. Our first act of free will is to believe in free will itself; this belief makes a practical difference in how we live.

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Key Contribution |
|------|------|------------------|
| The Principles of Psychology | 1890 | Foundational text of American psychology; introduces stream of consciousness, habit, emotion theory |
| The Will to Believe | 1897 | Defends the right to believe in cases of genuine, live, forced, momentous options |
| The Varieties of Religious Experience | 1902 | Pioneering study of religious experience from psychological perspective; defends religion's validity |
| Pragmatism | 1907 | Popular lectures defining pragmatism as a theory of truth and method for settling metaphysical disputes |
| Essays in Radical Empiricism | 1912 | Posthumous collection presenting his metaphysics of pure experience |

## Connections to Other Thinkers

- **Charles Sanders Peirce**: Co-founder of pragmatism. Peirce originated the pragmatic maxim, which James popularized and transformed. Peirce later distanced himself from James's version, calling his own view "pragmaticism."

- **John Dewey**: James's student and fellow pragmatist who developed instrumentalism. Together they established pragmatism as America's distinctive philosophical contribution.

- **Henri Bergson**: James admired Bergson's philosophy of duration and elan vital, finding parallels with his own stream of consciousness and pluralism. They corresponded and mutually influenced each other.

- **Edmund Husserl**: James's Principles of Psychology influenced Husserl's development of phenomenology, particularly the analysis of intentionality and the flow of consciousness.
