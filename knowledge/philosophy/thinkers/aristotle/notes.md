**Key Findings**:

1. **The Lyceum as Research Institution**: The Lyceum wasn't just a school but a major research center. They conducted systematic empirical investigations across dozens of fields. This institutional model influenced later universities and academies.

2. **Darwin's Praise**: Charles Darwin called Aristotle one of his intellectual gods – the father of biology and systematic observation of nature. Aristotle dissected over 50 species and distinguished cetaceans from fish millennia before modern biology.

3. **The Escape from Athens**: Aristotle's flight from Athens – "lest the Athenians sin twice against philosophy" – connects him directly to Socrates' fate. Both were charged with impiety, but Aristotle chose exile over martyrdom.

4. **The Organon**: Aristotle's logical works (collectively called the Organon, "tool") provided the framework for Western reasoning for over 2000 years. Kant claimed that logic had not advanced a single step since Aristotle.

5. **Potentiality/Actuality**: The dynamis/energeia distinction is Aristotle's central metaphysical contribution. Everything that exists is either potentially or actually something. Change is the actualization of what exists potentially.

## Reflections

### 2025-12-31 - On Potentiality and Emergence

Aristotle's potentiality/actuality framework provides a classical vocabulary for discussing emergence. When Krakauer speaks of "problem-solving matter" or when Friston describes self-organization, they're describing what Aristotle called the actualization of potential.

The key difference: Aristotle's teleology is intrinsic (the acorn aims at becoming an oak), while modern accounts often reduce teleology to selection effects (the oak-producing acorns were selected). But perhaps Aristotle's insight survives in attenuated form: complex adaptive systems do exhibit goal-directed behavior, even if that behavior emerges from mechanistic processes.

### On Habit and Character Formation

"We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts." This insight anticipates modern understanding of neural plasticity and habit formation. We are literally shaped by our repeated actions – both neurologically and characterologically.

This connects to Joscha Bach's computational view: the mind is software that modifies itself through its own activity. Aristotle would agree, though he'd resist reducing this to mere mechanism.
