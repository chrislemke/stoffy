# Thinker Profile: Friedrich Nietzsche

**Type**: Philosopher, Philologist
**Era**: Modern (1844-1900)
**Traditions**: Existentialism, Perspectivism, Continental Philosophy

## Core Ideas

- **Will to Power**: The fundamental drive of all life is not self-preservation or pleasure but the expansion and expression of power - the will to overcome, create, and transform. This is the basic force underlying all phenomena.

- **The Ubermensch (Overman)**: A vision of human potential beyond current limitations. The Ubermensch creates their own values, embraces life fully, and represents humanity's self-overcoming in the wake of nihilism.

- **Eternal Return**: The thought experiment of whether you could affirm your life if you had to live it exactly the same way for eternity. This serves as the ultimate test of life-affirmation (amor fati - love of fate).

- **Master-Slave Morality**: A genealogical distinction between life-affirming "master" morality (noble, creative, self-determining) and reactive "slave" morality (resentment-based, other-negating). Christianity exemplifies slave morality.

- **Death of God**: The cultural diagnosis that traditional religious and metaphysical foundations have lost their binding power. This creates the crisis of nihilism but also the opportunity for new value-creation.

- **Perspectivism**: There are no absolute facts, only interpretations from various perspectives. All knowledge is perspectival, shaped by the drives and interests of the knower. This is not relativism but a call for multiplying perspectives.

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Key Contribution |
|------|------|------------------|
| The Birth of Tragedy | 1872 | Introduces the Apollonian/Dionysian duality; argues Greek tragedy emerged from their tension |
| Thus Spoke Zarathustra | 1883-85 | Literary-philosophical masterpiece presenting the Ubermensch, eternal return, and will to power through the prophet Zarathustra |
| Beyond Good and Evil | 1886 | Critique of traditional morality and philosophy; develops perspectivism and master-slave distinction |
| On the Genealogy of Morality | 1887 | Historical analysis of the origins of moral concepts; introduces ressentiment and ascetic ideals |
| Twilight of the Idols | 1888 | Concise critique of Western philosophy and culture; attacks Socrates, Plato, Kant, and Christianity |

## Connections to Other Thinkers

- **Arthur Schopenhauer**: Nietzsche was initially captivated by Schopenhauer's philosophy but eventually rejected his pessimism. He transformed Schopenhauer's "will to live" into the "will to power" and replaced denial of life with affirmation.

- **Immanuel Kant**: Nietzsche criticized Kant's categorical imperative as disguised slave morality and attacked the thing-in-itself as a remnant of theological thinking. Yet he inherited Kant's critical project.

- **Martin Heidegger**: Heidegger devoted extensive work to Nietzsche, seeing him as the culmination of Western metaphysics. Nietzsche's influence on Heidegger's concepts of authenticity and nihilism is profound.

- **Michel Foucault**: Foucault's genealogical method of analyzing power/knowledge directly derives from Nietzsche's genealogy of morals. Nietzsche's perspectivism also influenced poststructuralism broadly.
