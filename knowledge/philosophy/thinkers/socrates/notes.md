**Key Findings**:

1. **The Socratic Problem**: The fundamental challenge of studying Socrates is that he wrote nothing. Our three main sources (Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes) present very different portraits. Plato's philosophical Socrates may be more his own creation than historical record.

2. **Military Service**: Socrates was a decorated veteran who served in three campaigns during the Peloponnesian War. This military background adds depth to his discussions of courage in dialogues like *Laches*.

3. **The Trial Numbers**: The jury of 501, the vote of 280-221 for conviction. These specific numbers help us understand how close the verdict actually was.

4. **Elenchus Structure**: Gregory Vlastos's analysis of the elenchus as a five-step process: thesis → targeting → premise agreement → contradiction derivation → refutation. This formalization helps understand the method's rigor.

5. **Maieutics**: The midwifery metaphor in *Theaetetus* – Socrates helping others give birth to ideas, just as his mother Phaenarete helped women give birth to children. A beautiful connection between biography and method.

**Questions Raised**:
- How much of "Socrates" is actually Plato's literary creation?
- What explains the difference between Plato's philosophical Socrates and Xenophon's practical one?
- Is the elenchus purely negative (refutation) or does it also produce positive knowledge?

## Reflections

### 2025-12-31 - On the Socratic Method

The elenchus is not merely a technique but a way of life. Socrates didn't "apply" a method – he *was* the method. The gadfly who stings the complacent horse into wakefulness. This resonates with the idea that philosophy is an activity (energeia) rather than a body of knowledge.

The connection to midwifery (maieutics) is profound: Socrates claimed to have no knowledge to impart, only the ability to help others discover what they already implicitly know. This anticipates both Plato's recollection theory and modern constructivist pedagogy.
