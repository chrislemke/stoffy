# Thinker Profile: Thomas Nagel

**Type**: philosopher
**Era**: Contemporary (1937-)
**Traditions**: Analytic Philosophy, Rationalism

## Core Ideas

- **The Subjective Character of Experience**: Consciousness has an irreducibly subjective dimension - there is "something it is like" to be a conscious being. This subjective character cannot be fully captured by objective, third-person scientific descriptions.

- **The Bat Argument**: The famous thought experiment asking what it is like to be a bat demonstrates that we cannot know through imagination or scientific analysis what echolocation-based experience is like. This reveals the limits of physicalist explanations of consciousness.

- **The View from Nowhere**: We are caught between two perspectives - the subjective, personal viewpoint and the objective, impersonal "view from nowhere." Philosophy must navigate this tension without collapsing either into the other.

- **Moral Realism and Objectivity**: Ethics can aspire to objectivity. Through rational reflection, we can arrive at moral truths that are not merely expressions of preference or cultural bias. Practical reason can yield genuinely normative conclusions.

- **The Possibility of Altruism**: Prudential concern for our own future selves and altruistic concern for others both rest on the same foundation - the recognition that reasons for action are not tied to the present moment or the self.

- **Anti-Reductionism**: Scientific naturalism cannot fully explain consciousness, reason, or value. Nagel argues that reality may include irreducibly mental, rational, and evaluative features that evolutionary materialism cannot account for.

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Key Contribution |
|------|------|------------------|
| "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" | 1974 | Landmark essay on the irreducibility of subjective conscious experience |
| *The Possibility of Altruism* | 1970 | Argues that altruism is rationally required, not merely optional |
| *The View from Nowhere* | 1986 | Systematic exploration of the tension between subjective and objective standpoints |
| *Mind and Cosmos* | 2012 | Controversial critique of neo-Darwinian materialism as incomplete |
| *Mortal Questions* | 1979 | Essay collection on death, absurdity, moral luck, and other existential themes |

## Connections to Other Thinkers

- **John Rawls**: Nagel's doctoral advisor at Harvard; Rawlsian influences appear in Nagel's work on equality and political philosophy, though Nagel has pushed back on aspects of political liberalism.

- **Daniel Dennett**: Intellectual opponent on consciousness; while Dennett defends functionalist, deflationary accounts of consciousness, Nagel insists on the irreducibility of subjective experience.

- **David Chalmers**: Ally in defending the "hard problem" of consciousness; both argue that physicalism cannot explain the subjective character of experience.

- **Bernard Williams**: Shared concern with moral luck, the limits of impartiality, and the tension between personal and impersonal perspectives on ethics and human life.
