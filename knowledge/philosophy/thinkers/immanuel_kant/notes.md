**Key Findings**:

1. **The Silent Decade**: Between 1770 and 1781, Kant published almost nothing while developing his revolutionary system. The *Critique of Pure Reason* emerged fully formed from this period of intense private work.

2. **The "Königsberg Clock"**: Kant's strict daily routine became legendary. He rose at 5 AM, worked until noon, took lunch, then walked his famous route so punctually that locals set their clocks by him. Only one event ever disrupted this routine: reading Rousseau's *Emile*.

3. **Quote Verification**: Many popular "Kant quotes" are actually paraphrases or misattributions. "Science is organized knowledge, wisdom is organized life" is actually Will Durant's summary, not Kant's words. Always verify against primary sources with Bekker numbers.

4. **Single Portrait**: The only portrait in Kant's study was of Rousseau. This aesthetic choice reveals how deeply Rousseau influenced his moral philosophy – the idea of human dignity and autonomy.

5. **Final Words**: "Es ist gut" ("It is good") – a fitting end for the philosopher who distinguished phenomena from things-in-themselves, accepting death with characteristic equanimity.

## Reflections

### 2025-12-31 - On Kantian Roots of Predictive Processing

The most striking connection in contemporary philosophy of mind is between Kant's transcendental idealism and predictive processing theory. Both hold that the mind actively structures experience rather than passively receiving it. Kant's forms of intuition (space and time) and categories of understanding are the brain's priors. The phenomenal world is a prediction, not a passive reflection of reality.

This is what Hohwy and others call "computational Kantianism." The categories and forms of intuition ARE the brain's generative model. The noumenal realm is the unconstructed reality that we can never directly access – only infer through prediction error.

### On the Königsberg Clock

There's something profound about Kant's legendary routine. A life of absolute regularity allowed him to produce philosophy of absolute depth. The external order freed his internal world for unlimited exploration. Perhaps the strictest constraints enable the greatest freedom – a paradox Kant himself might appreciate.
