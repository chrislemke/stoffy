# The Hidden Spring: A Journey to the Source of Consciousness

**Author**: Mark Solms
**Type**: Book
**Year**: 2021
**Publisher**: Profile Books / W. W. Norton
**Pages**: 448
**Status**: reading
**Rating**: 5/5

---

## Summary

Mark Solms presents what he calls the culmination of his lifelong quest to understand consciousness, offering a radical departure from mainstream neuroscience. His central thesis overturns the longstanding assumption that consciousness arises in the cerebral cortex. Instead, Solms locates the "hidden spring" of consciousness deep within the brainstem, specifically in evolutionarily ancient structures like the reticular activating system (RAS) and the periaqueductal grey (PAG). This is not merely an anatomical quibble but a fundamental reconceptualization: consciousness is not primarily about perception or cognition but about feeling. The most basic form of awareness is affective, not representational.

The book synthesizes three distinct intellectual traditions that rarely converse: the clinical insights of psychoanalysis (particularly Freud's incomplete biological project), the discoveries of affective neuroscience pioneered by Jaak Panksepp, and the mathematical formalism of Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle. Solms trained in both neuropsychology and psychoanalysis, founding the field of neuropsychoanalysis to bridge these long-separated domains. He sees himself as completing Freud's abandoned attempt to ground psychological concepts in neural mechanisms, but with crucial corrections: Freud got the relationship between id and ego inverted. The brainstem (id) does not merely supply raw energy to be organized by the cortex (ego); rather, the brainstem generates the conscious feelings that the cortex serves and elaborates.

The Free Energy Principle provides Solms with a formal framework for understanding why consciousness exists at all. Self-organizing systems must minimize their "free energy" (roughly, prediction error or uncertainty) to maintain themselves within viable physiological bounds. Consciousness arises precisely at the point where this homeostatic imperative becomes felt: deviation from optimal states is experienced as unpleasure, while returning toward them is experienced as pleasure. This grounds the hard problem in biology: consciousness is not an inexplicable addition to physical processes but the subjective aspect of self-regulation in complex organisms. We feel because feeling is how we monitor our own viability in situations that cannot be handled automatically.

The implications extend beyond academic neuroscience. If consciousness is fundamentally about embodied self-regulation rather than information processing, then artificial intelligence systems that lack genuine homeostatic imperatives may never achieve consciousness no matter how sophisticated their cognitive capabilities. Solms is currently leading a project to test this hypothesis by attempting to engineer an artificial consciousness based on free energy principles, a system that can genuinely feel its own states. He acknowledges the ethical dangers of such research, comparing it to nuclear physics in its potential for both profound insight and profound harm. The Hidden Spring thus offers not just a theory of consciousness but a research program and a warning.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Affect as the Core of Consciousness

**Claim**: The most fundamental form of consciousness is not perception, thought, or representation but feeling. Affective experience is the irreducible substrate upon which all other forms of awareness are built. You cannot have a feeling that you do not feel; therefore, feeling is inherently conscious.

**Evidence**:
- Electrical stimulation of brainstem structures reliably elicits intense emotional states
- Most psychiatric medications act on brainstem neurotransmitter systems
- Patients can lose massive amounts of cortex while retaining affective responsiveness
- Decorticate animals become more rather than less emotionally reactive
- Perception can occur unconsciously (as demonstrated in blindsight and subliminal priming), but affects cannot

**Implication**: Consciousness research has been misled by its focus on perception and cognition. The "easy problems" of consciousness (neural correlates of visual awareness, attention, memory) are downstream from the truly foundational question: why does anything feel like anything at all? By locating consciousness in affect, Solms offers a different entry point into the hard problem.

### 2. Brainstem Consciousness

**Claim**: The source of consciousness is not the cerebral cortex but the upper brainstem, specifically the reticular activating system (RAS) and periaqueductal grey (PAG). The cortex provides contents and cognitive elaboration, but consciousness itself originates in these evolutionarily ancient structures.

**Evidence**:
- Tiny lesions to the brainstem RAS produce coma; massive cortical damage does not
- Hydranencephalic children (born without a cortex) display emotional responsiveness, social engagement, and what appears to be sentient behavior
- The PAG is where all affective circuitry converges; it is the "decision triangle" for action selection
- No organism lacking these brainstem structures has ever been suspected of being conscious
- The RAS functions as an "on/off switch" for consciousness

**Implication**: The "cortical fallacy" has dominated consciousness science for too long. By recognizing that the cortex becomes conscious only to the extent it is aroused by the brainstem, we reframe the relationship between thinking and feeling: cognition serves affect, not the reverse.

### 3. Homeostatic Feelings

**Claim**: Feelings obey the laws of homeostasis, the basic mechanism by which living things maintain themselves within physiologically viable bounds. Feelings are the subjective registration of how well or poorly the organism is doing relative to its survival requirements.

**Evidence**:
- All affects can be mapped onto a pleasure-unpleasure dimension
- Deviations from homeostatic setpoints feel bad; returns toward them feel good
- The brainstem nuclei that generate consciousness are the same ones that regulate homeostasis
- Emotional disorders respond to drugs that modulate homeostatic neurotransmitter systems
- Panksepp's seven primary emotional systems (SEEKING, RAGE, FEAR, LUST, CARE, PANIC/GRIEF, PLAY) all serve biological imperatives

**Implication**: Consciousness has a biological function: it enables flexible behavioral regulation in novel situations that cannot be handled by automatic mechanisms. Feelings are not epiphenomenal additions to behavior but the means by which organisms navigate uncertainty.

### 4. Free Energy and Consciousness

**Claim**: The Free Energy Principle (FEP) provides a mathematical formalization of homeostasis. Conscious feelings are the subjective aspect of free energy dynamics: pleasure is the decrease in expected uncertainty; unpleasure is its increase.

**Evidence**:
- Self-organizing systems must minimize prediction error to maintain their structural integrity
- The same brainstem mechanisms that generate consciousness also perform free energy minimization
- Active inference explains how organisms proactively engage with the environment to reduce uncertainty
- Precision optimization (adjusting the signal-to-noise ratio of predictions) is associated with affective states
- The FEP unifies perception, action, and affect within a single formal framework

**Implication**: The hard problem is not a metaphysical mystery but a question of understanding why self-regulation in complex systems must be felt. Consciousness is the "vehicle by which the system monitors and thereby maintains its functional and structural integrity."

### 5. Extended Consciousness

**Claim**: While core consciousness is affective and brainstem-based, extended consciousness (involving self-awareness, autobiographical memory, and reflective thought) requires cortical elaboration. The cortex provides the "contents" that affective consciousness illuminates.

**Evidence**:
- Patients with cortical damage lose specific cognitive capacities (language, spatial reasoning, episodic memory) while retaining affective awareness
- Perceptual consciousness requires both brainstem arousal and cortical processing
- The cortex acts as a "virtual reality generator," using past experience to predict current sensory input
- Cognitive functions can occur unconsciously; affective functions cannot

**Implication**: There is a hierarchy of consciousness: affect is primary, perception is secondary, cognition is tertiary. Understanding this hierarchy transforms our approach to both normal psychology and psychopathology.

## Central Argument

The central argument of The Hidden Spring can be summarized in a single claim: **consciousness is affect, and affect is the subjective aspect of homeostasis**. This deceptively simple formulation has profound implications.

Solms begins by dismantling what he calls the "cortical fallacy"—the assumption that consciousness must arise in the cerebral cortex because that is where complex cognition occurs. Clinical evidence tells a different story. Patients with massive cortical lesions retain emotional responsiveness and the sense of being someone. Hydranencephalic children, born without a cortex, show clear signs of affective awareness. Meanwhile, even tiny lesions to the brainstem reticular activating system produce coma. The cortex, it turns out, becomes conscious only when aroused by the brainstem.

But why does anything feel like anything at all? This is Chalmers' hard problem, and Solms addresses it directly. His answer draws on Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle: self-organizing systems must maintain themselves within viable bounds by minimizing prediction error. In simple organisms, this happens automatically. But complex organisms face novel situations where automatic responses are insufficient. They need a way to monitor their own states and adjust their behavior flexibly. That is what feelings provide.

Consciousness, on this account, is the subjective registration of the gap between how we expect the world to be and how it actually is. When our predictions are violated in ways that threaten homeostasis, we feel unpleasure. When we successfully reduce uncertainty and return toward viable states, we feel pleasure. Feelings are not epiphenomenal; they are how complex organisms navigate a world that cannot be fully predicted in advance.

This framework has several key features:

1. **It naturalizes consciousness**: Feelings evolved because they enhance survival, not because of any mysterious metaphysical principle.

2. **It locates consciousness anatomically**: The brainstem structures that generate affect are the same ones that perform homeostatic regulation.

3. **It formalizes consciousness mathematically**: The Free Energy Principle provides equations that describe the dynamics of self-organizing systems.

4. **It addresses the hard problem**: Consciousness is not an inexplicable addition to physics but the inevitable subjective aspect of self-regulation in systems complex enough to require flexible behavioral control.

Solms does not claim to have dissolved the hard problem entirely. The step from mathematical description to phenomenal experience remains puzzling. But he argues that by grounding consciousness in affect rather than cognition, and by locating affect in the biology of homeostasis, we have a much more tractable problem than Chalmers supposed.

## My Response

### Philosophical Significance

The Hidden Spring represents one of the most serious attempts to address the hard problem of consciousness from within naturalistic science. What distinguishes Solms from many other neuroscientists writing about consciousness is his willingness to engage directly with the philosophical dimension of the problem rather than dismissing it or redefining it away.

His key move is to shift the explanandum: instead of asking why perception or cognition feels like something, he asks why homeostatic self-regulation feels like something. This reframing is genuinely illuminating. It connects consciousness to the deepest imperative of all living things—the drive to persist—and grounds phenomenal experience in biology rather than information processing.

The integration of Freudian concepts with contemporary neuroscience is more than nostalgia. Psychoanalysis was always concerned with affect, motivation, and the biological roots of mental life. By translating Freud's structural model (id/ego/superego) into neural terms, Solms recovers insights that were lost when behaviorism and cognitivism marginalized the study of feeling.

### Tensions and Questions

Several tensions remain unresolved:

1. **The explanatory gap persists**: Even if we accept that consciousness is the subjective aspect of free energy minimization, we still face the question of why there should be a subjective aspect at all. Solms acknowledges this but suggests the problem is more tractable than Chalmers supposed. I am not fully convinced.

2. **The brainstem thesis is contested**: Anil Seth, Antonio Damasio, and others agree that affect is central to consciousness but locate affective processing in subcortical forebrain structures (amygdala, insula, cingulate cortex) rather than the brainstem alone. The hydranencephalic children evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.

3. **Consciousness vs. affect**: Is all consciousness affective? The claim that "you cannot have a feeling that you do not feel" is true by definition, but it does not obviously follow that all consciousness reduces to feeling. What about detached contemplation or abstract mathematical reasoning?

4. **The Free Energy Principle**: The FEP is mathematically elegant but its explanatory status remains contested. Is it an empirical theory that could be falsified, or a framework that can accommodate any observation?

### Personal Impact

Reading Solms has shifted my thinking in several ways:

- I now take the primacy of affect more seriously than before. The phenomenology supports this: we do seem to feel our way through life before thinking about it.
- The connection between consciousness and biological survival feels right. Consciousness is not a luxury or an accident but something that matters for the organism.
- The critique of cortical-centric theories is compelling. We have been so impressed by the cortex's cognitive capacities that we may have overlooked the older structures that make cognition conscious at all.

At the same time, I remain uncertain about whether Solms has really addressed the hard problem or simply relocated it. The gap between third-person description (free energy dynamics) and first-person experience (what it feels like) does not seem to be bridged by appeal to homeostasis alone.

### Connection to Other Thinkers

Solms explicitly positions himself in relation to several intellectual traditions:

- **Antonio Damasio**: Both emphasize embodiment, interoception, and the primacy of feeling. Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis is a precursor to Solms's account. The main difference is anatomical: Damasio sees the insula and cingulate cortex as crucial; Solms emphasizes the brainstem.

- **Karl Friston**: The Free Energy Principle provides the formal framework. Solms and Friston have collaborated directly, co-authoring the foundational 2018 paper "The Hard Problem of Consciousness and the Free Energy Principle."

- **Jaak Panksepp**: Affective neuroscience is the experimental foundation. Panksepp's taxonomy of primary emotions (SEEKING, RAGE, FEAR, LUST, CARE, PANIC/GRIEF, PLAY) structures Solms's account of what consciousness actually feels like.

- **Sigmund Freud**: Solms sees himself as completing Freud's project, but with a crucial inversion: the id (brainstem) is the conscious component; the ego (cortex) provides unconscious cognitive elaboration.

## Criticisms and Limitations

### The Cortical Consciousness Objection

The strongest criticism of Solms comes from mainstream neuroscience, which holds that cortical networks are necessary for consciousness. Anil Seth notes that "recovery from coma and the vegetative state is best tracked by what happens in cortical networks," not brainstem activity alone. The role of the thalamocortical system in generating conscious contents is well-established.

Solms's evidence from hydranencephalic children is contested. While these children do display emotional behaviors, critics argue that "the behavioral signs he interprets as evidence of consciousness may have other explanations." We cannot directly access their phenomenology, and the inference from behavior to consciousness is always indirect. Assuming they are conscious is no more justified than assuming they are not.

Additionally, if Solms is correct that consciousness arose twice in evolution (brainstem affect and cortical perception), this would be an unusual case of convergent evolution for such a fundamental function.

### Hard Problem Considerations

Solms claims to address the hard problem, but many philosophers remain skeptical. His account explains the function of consciousness (homeostatic regulation) and its neural correlates (brainstem structures), but the explanatory gap between physical description and phenomenal experience persists.

The Free Energy Principle provides a formal framework, but formalisms describe rather than explain. We can write equations for free energy dynamics without those equations feeling like anything. The step from "free energy minimization" to "feeling of unpleasure" is not made transparent by the mathematics.

As one reviewer notes, "although Solms simplifies the Free Energy Principle, the connection between its mathematical formalism and subjective affect remains only partially clarified."

### Empirical Challenges

Several empirical claims are contested:

1. **The specificity of brainstem lesions**: While RAS lesions do produce coma, this may reflect disruption of arousal rather than consciousness per se. The distinction between arousal and awareness is debated.

2. **Precision optimization and consciousness**: Solms identifies precision optimization with consciousness, but in the predictive processing literature, precision is typically associated with attention rather than consciousness. These are related but not identical phenomena.

3. **The role of the cortex**: Even if core affect is brainstem-based, cortical processing may be necessary for the rich, differentiated conscious experiences that characterize human mental life. The relationship between "core consciousness" and "extended consciousness" requires more clarification.

### Theoretical Tensions

1. **Neuropsychoanalysis**: Many scientists remain skeptical of any rehabilitation of Freudian concepts. The criticism of behaviorism for removing first-person experience from psychology is fair, but it does not automatically validate psychoanalytic theory.

2. **Artificial consciousness**: Solms claims his theory makes artificial consciousness engineerable, but this remains speculative. The project to build an artificial conscious system is ongoing, and its success or failure will be crucial for evaluating the theory.

3. **Evolutionary plausibility**: If brainstem structures are sufficient for consciousness, then consciousness may be more widespread in the animal kingdom than typically assumed. This has implications for animal ethics but also raises questions about the adaptiveness of consciousness if it is so easily realized.

## Reading Notes

### 2025-12-27 - Comprehensive Analysis

Conducted extensive research on The Hidden Spring through academic papers, book reviews, and interviews. Key findings:

**On the brainstem thesis**: This is the most controversial aspect of the book. While Solms presents compelling clinical evidence (hydranencephalic children, RAS lesions), the mainstream neuroscience community remains skeptical. The debate centers on whether these cases demonstrate consciousness or merely unconscious emotional reflexes.

**On the Free Energy Principle**: Solms provides one of the most accessible introductions to the FEP, though the mathematical details remain challenging. The connection between FEP and phenomenal consciousness is the weakest link in the argument.

**On neuropsychoanalysis**: The attempt to rehabilitate Freudian concepts is met with mixed reception. The reframing of id/ego in neural terms is intriguing but relies heavily on the brainstem thesis.

**On artificial consciousness**: Solms's ongoing project to engineer artificial consciousness will be a crucial test of the theory. If successful, it would provide strong support; if not, the theory's core claims remain intact but less testable.

**Personal assessment**: The Hidden Spring is a genuinely important contribution to consciousness studies, regardless of whether its specific claims prove correct. The emphasis on affect, embodiment, and biological function represents a necessary corrective to overly cognitive approaches.
