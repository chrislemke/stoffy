# Responsibility and Control: A Theory of Moral Responsibility

**Author**: John Martin Fischer & Mark Ravizza, S.J.
**Type**: Book (Cambridge Studies in Philosophy and Law)
**Year**: 1998
**Publisher**: Cambridge University Press
**Pages**: viii + 277
**Status**: reading

---

## Summary

*Responsibility and Control* stands as one of the most systematic and influential treatments of moral responsibility in contemporary philosophy. Fischer and Ravizza develop what has become known as a "reasons-responsive compatibilist" account, arguing that moral responsibility does not require the ability to do otherwise (what they call "regulative control") but rather requires only "guidance control" - the capacity to guide one's actions through a mechanism that is both appropriately reasons-responsive and genuinely one's own. This represents a significant departure from traditional libertarian accounts that tie responsibility to alternative possibilities, and even from earlier compatibilist views that focused primarily on the agent's desires or deliberative processes.

The book's central innovation lies in its two-dimensional analysis of guidance control. First, the mechanism that produces action must exhibit what Fischer and Ravizza call "moderate reasons-responsiveness" - it must be receptive to reasons in a regular, understandable pattern and capable of reacting to at least some sufficient reasons to do otherwise. This notion is carefully calibrated: strong reasons-responsiveness (responding to all sufficient reasons) would be too demanding, while weak reasons-responsiveness (responding to at least one reason somewhere in logical space) would be too permissive. Second, the mechanism must be the agent's own - the agent must have "taken responsibility" for it through a process of coming to see herself as an apt target of the reactive attitudes on the basis of how that mechanism operates. This ownership condition introduces a historical dimension to responsibility that pure "snapshot" theories lack.

Fischer and Ravizza's framework is explicitly designed to handle Frankfurt-style cases - thought experiments in which an agent lacks alternative possibilities due to the presence of a counterfactual intervener, yet intuitively remains morally responsible. By locating responsibility in guidance control rather than regulative control, they sidestep the challenge that such cases pose to the Principle of Alternative Possibilities. The agent in a Frankfurt case acts from her own reasons-responsive mechanism, and that is all that responsibility requires. Determinism, like the presence of a Frankfurt-style intervener, does not threaten guidance control; it merely eliminates regulative control, which was never necessary for responsibility in the first place.

The book also addresses manipulation cases with considerable sophistication. Fischer and Ravizza argue that what makes manipulation responsibility-undermining is not that it determines the agent's action, but that it produces action through a mechanism that is not genuinely the agent's own. The historical condition on mechanism ownership is designed precisely to rule out such cases: an agent who has been manipulated into having certain psychological structures has not come to own those structures through the appropriate developmental process. This response to manipulation arguments has been influential, though it remains contested, particularly by hard incompatibilists like Derk Pereboom who argue that the difference between manipulation and ordinary deterministic causation is merely a matter of degree, not of kind.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Guidance Control vs. Regulative Control

**Claim**: Moral responsibility requires guidance control, not regulative control. Guidance control is the kind of control involved in actually guiding one's behavior in a particular way; regulative control is the power to guide behavior in more than one direction - the ability to do otherwise.

**Evidence**: Frankfurt-style cases demonstrate that agents can be responsible even when they lack the ability to do otherwise. In these cases, an agent decides and acts on her own, but a counterfactual intervener would have ensured the same outcome had she shown signs of deciding otherwise. Since the intervener plays no actual role, the agent seems responsible, yet she lacked alternative possibilities. Fischer and Ravizza take these cases to show that access to alternatives is not necessary for moral responsibility.

**Implication**: If regulative control is not required for responsibility, then determinism's elimination of alternative possibilities does not threaten responsibility. This opens space for a robust compatibilism that does not depend on controversial analyses of "could have done otherwise."

---

### 2. Reasons-Responsiveness: Strong, Moderate, and Weak

**Claim**: Guidance control requires that the mechanism issuing in action be "moderately reasons-responsive" - neither so demanding as to require perfect rationality nor so permissive as to allow any minimal sensitivity to reasons.

**Evidence**: Fischer and Ravizza develop a three-tier framework:

- **Strong Reasons-Responsiveness**: A mechanism is strongly reasons-responsive if it would recognize and respond appropriately to any sufficient reason to do otherwise. This is too demanding because many intuitively responsible agents - including ordinary wrongdoers - fail to attend to sufficient reasons to act differently.

- **Weak Reasons-Responsiveness**: A mechanism is weakly reasons-responsive if there exists some possible world in which it responds to a sufficient reason to do otherwise. This is too permissive because the pattern of responsiveness might be too arbitrary and disconnected from rational agency.

- **Moderate Reasons-Responsiveness**: A mechanism is moderately reasons-responsive if it exhibits *regular reasons-receptivity* and at least *weak reasons-reactivity*. Regular receptivity means the mechanism shows an understandable pattern of recognizing reasons - accepting one reason as sufficient implies that a stronger reason would also be recognized. Weak reactivity means the mechanism would respond appropriately to at least some sufficient reason in some possible world.

**Implication**: The asymmetry between receptivity (which requires regularity) and reactivity (which requires only weak capacity) reflects the intuition that what matters most for responsibility is the agent's understanding of and connection to reasons, not necessarily her perfect responsiveness to them.

---

### 3. Mechanism Ownership and Taking Responsibility

**Claim**: For an agent to exhibit guidance control, the reasons-responsive mechanism must be her own. Ownership is established through a historical process of "taking responsibility" for the mechanism.

**Evidence**: Fischer and Ravizza argue that "taking responsibility" involves coming to hold certain dispositional beliefs about oneself - specifically, the belief that one is an appropriate target of reactive attitudes (gratitude, resentment, praise, blame) on the basis of how one's mechanism operates. These beliefs must be acquired in an appropriate way, not through manipulation or coercion.

Three conditions for taking responsibility:
1. The agent must see herself as the source of her behavior
2. The agent must believe that she is an apt target of the reactive attitudes
3. These beliefs must be based on evidence and acquired through an appropriate developmental process

**Implication**: This historical condition distinguishes agents who act from their own psychological structures from those who have been manipulated into having certain structures. It rules out cases where manipulation installs a reasons-responsive mechanism but the agent has not genuinely made that mechanism her own.

---

### 4. Historical Conditions and the Rejection of Snapshot Theories

**Claim**: Responsibility is an essentially historical property. Whether an agent is responsible depends not just on her psychological state at the time of action, but on how she came to be in that state.

**Evidence**: Fischer and Ravizza argue against "current time-slice models" of responsibility by appeal to cases of hypnosis, brainwashing, and direct brain stimulation. In these cases, agents may have all the right psychological properties at the moment of action, yet intuitively are not responsible because their psychology was acquired through responsibility-undermining processes.

**Implication**: This distinguishes Fischer and Ravizza from pure internalists like Harry Frankfurt, who treat free will and responsibility as "snapshot" properties. For Fischer and Ravizza, history matters - an agent manipulated last night differs from an agent who developed her character naturally, even if they are psychological duplicates at the moment of action.

---

### 5. Frankfurt-Style Cases and the Principle of Alternative Possibilities

**Claim**: Frankfurt-style cases successfully undermine the Principle of Alternative Possibilities (PAP), which states that an agent is morally responsible only if she could have done otherwise.

**Evidence**: In a classic Frankfurt case, an agent (Jones) deliberates and decides to perform some action (say, vote for candidate A). Unbeknownst to Jones, a neuroscientist (Black) is monitoring Jones's brain and is prepared to intervene if Jones shows any sign of deciding otherwise - but Jones never shows such signs, so Black never intervenes. Jones acts on his own, yet could not have done otherwise. Fischer and Ravizza take this to show that alternative possibilities are not necessary for responsibility.

**Implication**: By rejecting PAP, Fischer and Ravizza can maintain that determinism is compatible with responsibility. The challenge from determinism (that it eliminates alternative possibilities) is neutralized if such possibilities were never required for responsibility in the first place.

---

### 6. The Actual-Sequence Mechanism

**Claim**: What matters for responsibility is the character of the actual causal sequence leading to action, not the availability of alternative sequences.

**Evidence**: Fischer and Ravizza focus on the "mechanism" that actually produces the action. This mechanism is assessed for reasons-responsiveness and ownership. The existence or non-existence of alternative sequences is irrelevant - what matters is whether the actual mechanism has the right properties.

**Implication**: This "actual-sequence" approach is central to semicompatibilism. It shifts attention from modal questions (what the agent could have done) to questions about the actual causal history (what actually produced the action and whether it was the right kind of process).

---

## Central Argument

The logical structure of Fischer and Ravizza's argument can be reconstructed as follows:

**Premise 1**: Moral responsibility requires that the agent have the appropriate kind of control over her actions.

**Premise 2**: There are two kinds of control: regulative control (the ability to bring about different outcomes) and guidance control (the actual guidance of behavior through an appropriate mechanism).

**Premise 3**: Frankfurt-style cases show that regulative control is not necessary for moral responsibility - agents in such cases are intuitively responsible despite lacking alternative possibilities.

**Premise 4**: Therefore, the kind of control required for moral responsibility is guidance control, not regulative control.

**Premise 5**: Guidance control consists in two conditions:
- (a) The mechanism that produces the action must be moderately reasons-responsive
- (b) The mechanism must be the agent's own (she must have "taken responsibility" for it)

**Premise 6**: Both conditions (a) and (b) are compatible with causal determinism. Determinism does not prevent mechanisms from being reasons-responsive, nor does it prevent agents from taking responsibility for their mechanisms.

**Conclusion**: Therefore, moral responsibility is compatible with causal determinism. (Semicompatibilism)

## My Response

### Philosophical Significance

Fischer and Ravizza's *Responsibility and Control* represents one of the most important contributions to the free will debate in the late twentieth century. Their development of "semicompatibilism" - the view that moral responsibility is compatible with determinism regardless of whether free will is - offers a strategically brilliant move. By decoupling responsibility from both libertarian free will and from the question of determinism's truth, they create space for a stable moral practice that does not depend on resolving the deepest metaphysical puzzles.

The reasons-responsive framework has proven extraordinarily influential. It captures something intuitively important: that responsible agents are those who act for reasons and can be reached by reasons. An agent who would perform the same action regardless of any considerations - the truly obsessed, the hypnotized, the compulsive - lacks something crucial for responsibility. Fischer and Ravizza give this intuition a precise articulation.

### Tensions and Questions

Several tensions emerge upon careful study:

**The Mechanism Problem**: Fischer and Ravizza speak of "mechanisms" that produce action, but it remains unclear what individuates mechanisms. If mechanisms are typed too narrowly, then manipulation might produce a different mechanism-type than normal development, trivially satisfying the historical condition. If typed too broadly, then manipulation and normal development might produce the same mechanism-type, and the historical condition does no work.

**The Manipulation Challenge**: Derk Pereboom's "Four-Case Argument" poses a serious challenge. If we stipulate that a manipulated agent satisfies all of Fischer and Ravizza's conditions - including having taken responsibility through what appears to be an appropriate process - many still judge her not responsible. This suggests that the conditions may not be sufficient, or that the concept of "appropriate" acquisition is doing unexplained work.

**The Asymmetry Problem**: The asymmetry between receptivity (requiring regularity) and reactivity (requiring only weak capacity) is intriguing but potentially problematic. Why should we demand more of an agent's recognition of reasons than of her response to them? This seems to suggest that responsibility is primarily a matter of understanding rather than doing - a view with controversial implications.

**The Strawsonian Alternative**: Some critics prefer P.F. Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" approach, which treats reactive attitudes as explanatorily prior to the concept of responsibility. Fischer and Ravizza engage with Strawson but ultimately offer a more metaphysically committed view - they give conditions for responsibility rather than deflating the concept into social practice.

### Personal Impact

What strikes me most about Fischer and Ravizza's project is its ambition combined with its modesty. They aim to give necessary and sufficient conditions for moral responsibility - an audacious goal. Yet they are modest about the metaphysical stakes: they remain neutral on whether determinism is true and on whether "libertarian" free will exists. This allows their theory to function regardless of how the deep metaphysical questions are resolved.

The framework also connects beautifully to practical moral psychology. Thinking about which of my actions issue from my own reasons-responsive mechanisms, and which might be products of compulsion, addiction, or external manipulation, gives me a vocabulary for moral self-reflection. The question "Is this really me acting?" becomes the question "Have I taken responsibility for this mechanism? Is it responsive to reasons in the right way?"

### Connection to Determinism Debates

Fischer's "semicompatibilism" occupies a distinctive position in the landscape. Unlike traditional compatibilists, Fischer does not claim that determinism is compatible with free will in any robust sense. Unlike libertarians, he does not claim that we need indeterminism for responsibility. Unlike hard incompatibilists like Pereboom, he does not claim that determinism would undermine responsibility.

The position is strategically clever: it takes the thing we care about most (moral responsibility and the appropriateness of our reactive attitudes) and argues that it is safe from determinism. Whether we have "free will" in some thicker sense remains an open question, but it is not a question we need to answer in order to hold each other responsible.

## Philosophical Connections

### Harry Frankfurt
Fischer and Ravizza build directly on Frankfurt's seminal work on alternative possibilities. Frankfurt's 1969 paper "Alternate Possibilities and Moral Responsibility" provides the cases that motivate their rejection of regulative control. However, they depart from Frankfurt's pure internalism by adding the historical ownership condition.

### P.F. Strawson
Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" (1962) provides the conceptual framework of reactive attitudes that Fischer and Ravizza employ. They accept that being morally responsible involves being an appropriate target of reactive attitudes like resentment and gratitude. However, they give more substantive conditions for when such attitudes are appropriate, rather than treating the practice itself as self-justifying.

### Susan Wolf
Wolf's work on the "Reason View" of free will and her concept of the ability to act "in accordance with reason" parallels Fischer and Ravizza's emphasis on reasons-responsiveness. Her asymmetrical view (that praise and blame have different conditions) raises interesting questions for their more unified approach.

### Gary Watson
Watson's distinction between two faces of responsibility - the "attributability" face and the "accountability" face - informs Fischer and Ravizza's analysis. Watson has also been a critical interlocutor, questioning aspects of their mechanism-based framework.

### Derk Pereboom
Pereboom represents the primary foil for Fischer and Ravizza as a "hard incompatibilist." His Four-Case Argument attempts to show that there is no principled difference between manipulation and ordinary deterministic causation, threatening to undermine the historical condition's ability to save responsibility.

### Robert Kane
Kane defends libertarian free will with his account of "self-forming actions" (SFAs). For Kane, genuine responsibility requires that at some point in one's history, one made undetermined choices that formed one's character. Fischer and Ravizza's semicompatibilism stands in direct opposition to this requirement.

### Alfred Mele
Mele has developed sophisticated manipulation cases (including the "Beth" case of overnight brainwashing) that challenge Fischer and Ravizza's historical condition. If Beth is a psychological duplicate of Ann but lacks responsibility because of how she came to be that way, what exactly makes the difference?

## Criticisms and Limitations

### 1. The Manipulation Argument Challenge

Pereboom's Four-Case Argument presents a graduated series of manipulation scenarios, each designed to satisfy Fischer and Ravizza's conditions while eliciting the intuition that the agent is not responsible. The challenge is to identify a principled difference between manipulation and ordinary deterministic causation:

- Case 1: Scientists directly manipulate an agent's neural states
- Case 2: Scientists program the agent from birth
- Case 3: An agent is raised in a deterministic environment designed to produce certain values
- Case 4: An agent in an ordinary deterministic world

If we deny responsibility in Case 1, Pereboom argues, we should deny it in Case 4, since the differences are merely matters of degree. Fischer and Ravizza's appeal to historical conditions seems unable to locate a principled distinction.

### 2. Sourcehood Objections

Source incompatibilists argue that responsibility requires being the ultimate source of one's actions - and determinism is incompatible with ultimacy. Even if an agent acts from a reasons-responsive mechanism that she owns, if the causal chain stretches back before her birth to factors beyond her control, she is not the ultimate source. Fischer and Ravizza explicitly reject the ultimacy requirement, but critics argue this leaves responsibility mysteriously "floating free" of causal history.

### 3. Luck Objections to Reasons-Responsiveness

Critics have noted that on Fischer and Ravizza's view, whether an agent is responsible depends on counterfactual facts about how she would respond in other possible worlds. But why should responsibility depend on such facts? If an agent happens to have a mechanism that would respond to some reason in some possible world, but this fact plays no role in her actual action, its bearing on responsibility seems obscure.

### 4. The Mechanism Individuation Problem

How finely should mechanisms be individuated? If too fine-grained, then manipulation and normal development produce different mechanism-types by definition, trivializing the historical condition. If too coarse-grained, manipulation and normal development might count as the same mechanism, and the historical condition fails to rule out manipulation. Fischer and Ravizza have not fully resolved this challenge.

### 5. The Taking Responsibility Regress

For an agent to take responsibility for a mechanism, she must come to hold certain beliefs in an "appropriate" way. But what makes a way of acquiring beliefs appropriate? If it requires that the belief-acquisition process itself be one the agent owns and takes responsibility for, a regress threatens. Fischer and Ravizza need a stopping point that does not itself require prior responsibility.

### 6. The Psychopath Problem

Some critics have questioned whether moderate reasons-responsiveness captures what is lacking in psychopaths. A psychopath may be quite good at recognizing reasons (including moral reasons) while being unable or unwilling to respond to them appropriately. This challenges the asymmetry between receptivity and reactivity, since the psychopath may exhibit regular receptivity but deficient reactivity.
